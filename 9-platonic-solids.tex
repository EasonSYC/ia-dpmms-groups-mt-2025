\section{Platonic Solids}
\label{sec:platonic-solids}

Amazingly, while there are infinitely many regular 2-dimensional polygons, in 3 dimensions, there are only 5 polyhedrons, which are called \emph{platonic solids}.

\begin{definition}[Platonic Solid]
    \label{def:platonic-solid}%
    A convex polyhedron \(X \subseteq \RR^3\) is a \emph{platonic solid} if
    \begin{itemize}
        \item every face is a regular \(n\)-gon;
        \item \(G = \Isom\pqty{X}\) acts transitively on the faces; and
        \item if \(x \in X\) is the midpoint of a face, then
        \[
            \Stab_{G} \pqty{x} \isomorphic D_{2n}.
        \]
    \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

There are (up to similarity) 5 platonic solids, as shown in \autoref{fig:platonic-solids}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the \emph{tetrahedron} (4 triangular faces, 4 vertices);
    \item the \emph{cube/hexahedron} (6 square faces, 8 vertices);
    \item the \emph{octahedron} (8 triangular faces, 6 vertices);
    \item the \emph{dodecahedron} (12 pentagonal faces, 20 vertices); and
    \item the \emph{icosahedron} (20 triangular faces, 12 vertices).
\end{itemize}

Two solids \(X, Y\) are \emph{dual} if \(Y\) can be constructed from \(X\) by putting vertices in the centres of each face, and joining vertices in adjacent faces by edges.

\begin{examples*}[Dual Platonic Solids]
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The cube and the octahedron are dual, as shown in \autoref{fig:duality-cube-octahedron}.
        \item The tetrahedron is dual with itself.
        \item The dodecahedron and the icosahedron are dual.
    \end{itemize}
\end{examples*}

In particular, if \(X\) and \(Y\) are dual, then
\[
    \Isom\pqty{X} \isomorphic \Isom\pqty{Y}
\]
so we only 3 isometry groups to identity.

Before we continue, note that the isometry group of any such platonic solid \(G\) satisfies \(G \subgroup \Or\pqty{3}\), and hence \(G_0 = G \cap \SO\pqty{3}\) being the subgroup preserving orientation satisfies that
\[
    G_0 = \ker \pqty{\functiondomain{\rightbar{\det}_{G}}{G}{C_2}}
\]
and hence by \autoref{thm:first-isomorphism} (First Isomorphism Theorem)
\[
    \abs{G : G_0} = \img \pqty{\rightbar{\det}_{G}} \leq 2.
\]

For any platonic solid, there are reflectional symmetries which belong to \(\Or\pqty{3} \setminus \SO\pqty{3}\), so we must have
\[
    \abs{G : G_0} = 2
\]
and hence by \autoref{sec:lagranges-theorem} (Lagrange's theorem),
\[
    \abs{G} = 2 \abs{G_0}
\]

\begin{example*}[Symmetries of Tetrahedron]
    Let \(G = \Isom\pqty{\text{tetrahedron}}\). From \autoref{def:platonic-solid}, \(G\) acts transitively on the \(4\) surfaces, and each stabiliser has size \(6\), so by \autoref{cor:orbit-stabiliser} (Orbit--Stabiliser Theorem), we have
    \[
        \abs{G} = 4 \times 6 = 24.
    \]

    Furthermore, \(G\) acts on the \(4\) vertices, defining a homomorphism
    \[
        \functiondomain{\theta}{G}{S_4}.
    \]

    We prove that \(\theta\) is injective. Suppose for some \(g \in G\), that
    \[
        \theta\pqty{g} = e.
    \]

    Then \(g\) fixes the \(4\) vertices which are not co-planar, and hence by the 3-dimensional version (four point lemma) of \autoref{lem:three-point-lemma} (three point lemma), we have \(g = \identity\).
    
    Hence, \(\theta\) is injective, and by comparing sizes, we see
    \[
        G \isomorphic S_4.
    \]
\end{example*}

To identify the group of \emph{rotational symmetries}
\[
    G_0 = G \cap \SO\pqty{3}
\]
where the tetrahedron is centred on the origin, we conjecture \(G_0 \isomorphic A_4\).

\begin{lemma}[Uniqueness of Index 2 Subgroup of \(S_n\)]
    \label{lem:uniqueness-index-2-subgroup}
    If \(H \subgroup S_n\) and \(\abs{S_n : H} = 2\), then \(H = A_n\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Because \(\abs{S_n : H} = 2\), then \(H \subgroup S_n\) is normal, hence \(H \normalsub S_n\), and by taking quotients,
    \[
        \flatfrac{S_n}{H} \isomorphic C_2.
    \]

    We therefore have the quotient map as a surjective homomorphism
    \[
        \functiondomain{\theta}{S_n}{C_2 = \Bqty{\pm 1}}
    \]
    and hence \(\ker \theta = H\).

    Since transpositions generate \(S_n\) and \(\theta\) is surjective, there is some transposition \(\tau_0\) such that
    \[
        \theta \pqty{\tau_0} = -1.
    \]

    Since all transpositions are conjugate to \(\tau_0\), for any transposition \(\tau\),
    \[
        \tau = \sigma \tau_0 \sigma\inverse
    \]
    for some \(\sigma \in S_n\), then
    \begin{align*}
        \theta \pqty{\sigma \tau_0 \sigma\inverse} &= \theta\pqty{\sigma} \theta\pqty{\tau_0} \theta\pqty{\sigma}\inverse \\
        &= \theta\pqty{\tau_0} \\
        &= -1,
    \end{align*}
    so \(\theta\) and \(\sign\) (the sign homomorphism) agree on a generating set of \(S_n\), and hence they are identical, so
    \[
        H = \ker \theta = \ker \sign = A_n.
    \]
\end{proof}

Therefore, in particular, we have \(\abs{G : G_0} = 2\), so
\[
    G_0 \isomorphic A_4.
\]

\begin{example*}[Cube/Octahedron]
    Let \(G = \Isom\pqty{\text{cube}}\). Similarly, applying \autoref{cor:orbit-stabiliser} (Orbit--Stabiliser Theorem) on the midpoints of faces gives
    \[
        \abs{G} = 6 \times 8 = 48.
    \]

    In particular, the index 2 normal subgroup
    \[
        \abs{G_0} = 24.
    \]

    Notice that \(G \actson\) set of 4 long diagonals of the cube, so we obtain a homomorphism
    \[
        \functiondomain{\theta}{G_0}{S_4}.
    \]

    Instead, we show this time that \(\theta\) is surjective, and since transpositions generate \(S_4\) from \autoref{thm:transposition-generate}, it suffices to show that any transposition is contained in \(\img \theta\). Indeed, from \autoref{fig:diagonals-of-cube-and-transpositions}, we see that a rotation about an axis through midpoint of two opposite edges maps to a transposition under \(\theta\), and we get
    \[
        \binom{4}{2} = 6 = \frac{12}{2}
    \]
    transpositions, which are all of them. Hence, \(\img \theta = S_4\) indeed. By comparing sizes, we have \(G_0 \isomorphic S_4\).

    Now, notice that \(-\identityM \notin G_0\) but \(-\identityM \in G\) and commutes with everything in \(G_0 \isomorphic S_4\). So by \autoref{thm:dpt-direct-product-theorem} (Direct Product Theorem), we have
    \[
        G \isomorphic S_4 \times C_2.
    \]
\end{example*}

Finally, we examine the isometry group of a dodecahedron/icosahedron. (This is non-examinable in the course.)

\begin{example*}[Dodecahedron/Icosahedron]
    Let \(G = \Isom \pqty{\text{dodecahedron}}\) and \(G_0\) be the rotational subgroup with index 2.

    Similar to the examples above, we have
    \[
        \abs{G} = 12 \times 10 = 120, \abs{G_0} = 60.
    \]

    By drawing diagonals on the faces, as shown in \autoref{fig:cubes-in-dodecahedron}, we may inscribe 5 cubes into the dodecahedron. (This diagram shows one of them, using one diagonal on each of the \(12\) faces, and there are \(5\) diagonals in a pentagon, so there are \(5\) such cubes.)

    Since the 5 cubes are built symmetrically from the geometry of the dodecahedron, we see that
    \[
        G_0 \actson 5 \text{ cubes}.
    \]

    This leads to a homomorphism
    \[
        \functiondomain{\theta}{G_0}{S_5}.
    \]

    Now, notice rotation around an axis through opposite pair of vertices lead to a \(3\)-cycle in \(S_5\). There are \(10\) diagonals between opposite pairs of vertices, and we get \(10\) inverse pairs of \(3\)-cycles this way, which must then be all \(20\) \(3\)-cycles in \(S_5\).

    Since the set of \(3\)-cycles form a conjugacy class in \(A_5\), by Sheet 4, Q2, the subgroup generated by a conjugacy class is normal, and hence using the fact that \(A_5\) is simple, these \(3\)-cycles must then generate the whole of \(A_5\).

    This shows that
    \[
        A_5 \subgroup \img \theta,
    \]
    but we have
    \[
        60 = \abs{G_0} \geq \abs{\img \theta} \geq \abs{A_5} = 60
    \]
    so all the equal signs must be taken at the same time, and hence \(\theta\) is an injective homomorphism, with \(\img \theta = A_5\), and hence by \autoref{thm:first-isomorphism} (First Isomorphism Theorem), we have
    \[
        G_0 \isomorphic A_5.
    \]

    Similar to the cubes, \(-\identityM\) is a symmetry of the dodecahedron that commutes with everything. So \autoref{thm:dpt-direct-product-theorem} (Direct Product Theorem) implies that
    \[
        G \isomorphic A_5 \times C_2.
    \]
\end{example*}
